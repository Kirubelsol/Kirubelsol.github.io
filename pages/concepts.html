Filters Design: Details about the filters

Filters are circuit components that are significantly used in signal processing, electronics, and more fields. They are usually made of passive electrical components such as resistors and capacitors, or the passive components and an operational amplifier. These filters can pass or attenuate specific frequencies. Considering this, they can be used to refine signals such as in communcation system, reduce noise, and remove or enhance certain frequency ranges. As stated, there are two main types: Passive and Active filters.

Passive Filter

Passive filters are electronic circuits that use passive circuit componenets which don't generate power ,such as capacitors, resisitors, and inductors, to alter the magnitude and phase of the input signals to the circuit. This includes attenuating or permmiting specific frequency ranges of the input signals. The passive filters include low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, and band-stop filters. Some basic properties of passive filters include:

  • Uses passive componenets like resistors
  • Don't introduce energy gain or signal amplification
  • Don't require external power for operation
  • Simpler in design and cheaper compared to active filters
  • Limited in precision and ability to compensate for signal loss compared to active filters
  • Used for basic frequency response shaping

Active Filter

Passive filters are electronic circuits that use active components ,such as operational amplifiers, to alter the magnitude and phase of the input signals to the circuit. This includes attenuating or permmiting specific frequency ranges and amplifying the input signals. The active filters also include low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, and band-stop filters. Some basic properties of active filters include:

  • In addition to passive components, it uses active components like operational amplifiers
  • Allow for signal amplification and energy gain
  • Have external power requirements
  • Complex in design and more expensive than passive filters
  • High in precision and ability to compensate for signal loss compared to passive filters
  • Used in advanced filtering tasks
  • Offers better control over properties like frequency response and gain

The four common filters

Low-pass filter: passes signals that are lower than the desired cutoff frequency while attenuating the signals afterwards. It is usually used to element high frequency noise.

High-pass filter: attenuates signals that are lower than the desired cutoff frequency while passing the signals afterwards. It is usually used to remove low frequency noise.

Band-pass filter: passes signals within the desired frequency range while attenuating signals that are not in the range. It is used to isolate a desired range of frequencies.

Band-stop filter: rejects signal within a certain frequency range while passing signals that are not in the range. It is used to avoid unwanted frequencies in signals.

Common concepts and parameters used in explaining the filters

Operational amplifiers (Op-amps)

Operational amplifiers are electronic components used in analog circuit design for amplifying small input voltage differences across its differential inputs and allows for several signal processing applications. Their main properties are:

  • Differential inputs, which are the negative and positive terminals, to amplify the small voltage differences between them
  • Besides the two differential input, it has output terminal and two other terminals for DC power supply to operate the op-amp
  • Low output impedance to avoid significant voltage drop
  • High input impedance since in ideal case an op-amp shouldn't draw current from inputs.
  • Virtual short circuit meaning insignifcant voltage difference and ideally no current flow between the two input terminals due to high open-loop gain. For further calculation, we will assume virtual short across the terminals.

Impedance

Impedance is the opposition of alternating current flow through an AC circuit. It includes resistance, representing opposition of current in resistive manner, and reactance, representing the phase shift between voltage and current due to reactive components like capacitors and inductors.

  • Impedance of a resistor \( (Z_R) \): purely resistive and is equal to the resistance. \( Z_R = R \)
  • Impedance of a capacitor \( (Z_C) \): reactive and inversely related to frequency. \( Z_C = {1 \over {(2\pi fC)}} \)
  • Impedance of an inductor \( (Z_L) \): reactive and directly related to frequency. \( Z_L = 2\pi fL \)

Kirchhoff's laws and Voltage division

  • Kirchhoff's current law: states that the sum of current entering a junction is equal to the sum of current leaving the junction. In the figure to right, at the top, the current through \( R_1 \) is equal to the sum of current through \( R_2 \) and \( R_3 \).
    \( i_1 = i_2 + i_3 \)
  • Kirchhoff's voltage law: states that the sum of voltage across a closed loop is zero. In the bottom figure. \( V_i = V_C + V_R \) where \( V_C \) and \( V_R \) are the voltage across the capacitor and resistor respectively.
  • Voltage division: states that in series connection of components like resistors, the voltage drop across a component is proportiional to its impedance over the total circuit impedance. Kirchhoff's laws can be used to prove this. For instance, in the circuit to right:
    \( i_R = i_C = i\)       \( V_R = iR \)       \( V_C = iZ_c \)       \( V_i = i(R + Z_c) \)       \( i = {V_i \over {R+Z_c}} \)
    \( V_R = iR = V_i({R \over {R+Z_c}}) \)
    Hence the voltage across the resistor is proportional to the resistance over the total circuit impedance.

Common parameters that are computed for

Cutoff frequency: The cutoff frequency is the frequency at which the magnitude of \( Vout \over Vin \) is often defined to be below \( {-3dB =} {1 \over \sqrt{2}} \). In band-pass filter we have upper and lower cutoff frequencies. Cutoff frequency serves as the boundary between the frequencies that the filter allows to pass and attenuate. For instance in high-pass filter, below the cutoff frequency the magnitude of the signal starts to be signficantly attenuated.

Transfer function: a mathematical expression describing how the filter alters the magnitude and phase of the input signal helping us understand how the filter process the signals. The transfer function is used to come up with a bode plot to visualize the magnitude and phase change across frequencies.

Gain: indicates the amplification or attenuation factor applied to the input signal due to the filter at different frequencies.

Phase shift: refers to the phase difference introduced to the input signal due to the filter which indicates the shift in timing between the input and output signal measured in radians or degrees. signal within a certain frequency range while passing signals that are not in the range. It is used to avoid unwanted frequencies in signals.

Quality factor: a dimensionless parameter commonly used in band-pass and band-stop filters indicating the performance of the filter. It is the ratio of the center frequency to the bandwidth of the filter. Center frequency is the arithmentic mean of the upper and lower cutoff frequency or the geometric mean which is the square root of the cutoff frequencies product. Bandwidth refers to the range of frequencies that passes through in bandpass filter or that is attenuated in bandstop filters. A higher quality factor value indicates narrow pass band of frequencies making it highly selective while lower value indicates wider bandwidth region. A higher Q value filter can be used in precise frequency selection application while a lower value filter can be used broader frequency coverage.
